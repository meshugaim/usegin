## What "Definition of Good" Means Here

The phrase isn't explicitly spoken as "definition of good" — but the concept is fully present in Guy and Oria's framing. The team is grappling with:

**Current state:** What "good" means is self-evident — a response is noticeably better or worse, you see it with your eyes.

**Problem ahead:** Once you're tuning prompts and improvements are 3–5%, you lose that intuition. You can't eyeball whether prompt variant A is better than prompt variant B at that granularity.

**What evals do:** They formalize "definition of good" — encoding the criteria for what a high-quality Effi response looks like into a test harness that scores consistently. Without this definition, you can't measure marginal improvement, catch regressions, or hand optimization to Claude.

Oria's V0 is specifically about the **infrastructure** that makes this measurable: a way to run a prompt change against a dataset of test questions, score the outputs, and track the result over time.

---

## What "Matrices" Means

From Oria's line *"תעשה מטריצות"* ("will make matrices"):

In eval methodology, a **matrix** is a structured comparison grid — typically:
- Rows: test questions/prompts from real user sessions
- Columns: different prompt variants being evaluated
- Cells: scored quality of Effi's answer (e.g., accuracy, completeness, hallucination rate)

Oria's vision is that once evals v0 exists, you can hand the prompt-optimization loop entirely to Claude: Claude proposes a variant → runs it across the test matrix → scores it → decides whether to keep it. The "matrices" are both the test infrastructure and the automated improvement mechanism.
