## TOP — distilled IDF ma'amatz doctrine (≤500 words)

**Ma'amatz** (מאמץ — "effort") is the third M. Hebrew IDF doctrine distinguishes three operative forms inside any combat order (*pkudat krav*):

- **מאמץ ראשי** (*ma'amatz rashi*) — **main effort.** The single named act/sector/unit whose success the *parent's* end-state is staked on. Resources, fires, reserves, ISR, and risk-tolerance flow here first. *Schwerpunkt* in German doctrine, "main effort" in US/British FM 6-0 — the IDF inherited the concept from Auftragstaktik and absorbed it through the British 5-paragraph order.
- **מאמצי-עזר** (*ma'amtzei-ezer*) — **supporting efforts.** Plural. Every other named action in the same order. Their explicit job is *to set conditions for the ma'amatz rashi to succeed* — fix the enemy, deceive, screen, isolate, secure a flank, open a route. A supporting effort that "wins" while the main effort fails has lost the battle by definition.
- **מאמץ משני** / **כיבוש** (secondary or follow-on effort) — what becomes the new ma'amatz rashi *after* the original succeeds, or in a contingency branch.

**Three doctrinal claims that earn the term operationally:**

1. **Singularity.** There is exactly *one* ma'amatz rashi at a given echelon at a given time. "Two main efforts" is grammatically a confession that the commander hasn't decided. CALL's *Less is Better* and the Vandergriff/Schwerpunkt literature converge here: a plan with co-equal efforts is a plan with no Schwerpunkt, and under fog the un-prioritized branches starve each other rather than reinforce. (Wass de Czege, Vandergriff, Echevarria.)
2. **Resource asymmetry is the operational meaning.** Designating a ma'amatz rashi is *not* labeling — it is committing to *unequal allocation*: the main effort gets first call on fires, ISR, the reserve, the engineer assets, the risk budget. If two efforts get equal resources, neither is the main effort regardless of what the order says. This is where "main effort" stops being decoration and becomes a forcing function for prioritization. (FM 6-0 §B; ADP 3-0; Israeli operational orders embed this via the *תיעדוף* — *ti'eduf*, "prioritization" — line in the *pkudat krav*.)
3. **The main effort shifts; that shift is a command act, not drift.** Vandergriff (citing Wass de Czege citing the Bundeswehr): "The commander of each unit can change his Schwerpunkt at any time… if such a change will help him more easily achieve his commander's intent." The shift is announced — subordinates re-align supporting efforts to the new ma'amatz rashi the moment the order goes out. A main effort that shifts *silently* — because the original stalled and another succeeded by accident — is the failure mode the IDF tikkur catches. The post-1973 reforms made *named, deliberate shift of ma'amatz* a graded skill in officer evaluation. (IDF Dado Center; Shamir; ICGS.)

**The ma'amatz vs. mishima distinction.** *Mishima* names the *what* and *why* of one unit's mission. *Ma'amatz* names *where in the parent's plan this unit's mission sits in the priority stack*. Every unit at every echelon has a mishima; only one of the parent's children at a time is the ma'amatz rashi. A unit can have a textbook mishima and still execute *as* a supporting effort — the two layers are orthogonal.

**Vague-ma'amatz signature.** AAR-flagged failure modes: (a) the order names "the main effort" but allocates resources equally — *labeling without committing*; (b) the order names two main efforts — *no decision*; (c) the order omits the ma'amatz line entirely, leaving subordinates to infer priority from H-hour or geography — the 2006 Lebanon failure mode (*ke-ze ke-ze*, "this and that," instead of *zeh ha-ikar*, "this is the main thing"). All three are visible *before* execution, in the order itself.

### What we *already* carry that absorbs part of ma'amatz

- **Principle 05 #6 (Lihu's attention is COG)** carries the *strategic-level* Schwerpunkt: across all of UseGin, the one thing whose collapse collapses everything is Lihu's attention. This is correct and should not be touched. Ma'amatz is a *tactical-level* discipline that operates *under* this — within a single charter or spawn batch, which sub-action gets the priority?
- **Clausewitz R5** carries the *concept* (focal point that holds the system together) and the *ranking discipline* (improvements ranked by COG protected). This is correct. Ma'amatz extends it into *per-operation* shape: even if you've ranked types of work, you still have to designate, *for this batch*, which one is the main effort.
- **rnd skill's "Aharai" clause** says "spawn the load-bearing angle as Opus, routine ones as Haiku." This is *closely adjacent* to ma'amatz — Aharai is "strongest agent first into the unknown"; ma'amatz is "this angle is the main effort, allocate accordingly." Adjacent but not identical: Aharai picks model-class, ma'amatz picks priority among same-class agents.
- **Economy-of-force language** (Modern Application #6, Synthesis #11) is the *consequence* side of ma'amatz: once you've named the main effort, supporting efforts are economy-of-force (minimum committed to hold their job). We carry the conclusion without explicitly naming the premise.

### What's missing — the click

**The rnd skill currently spawns N parallel professors as peers with no main-effort designation.** The mishima professor's whiteboard already noticed Polls B and C ship without a purpose clause; the parallel finding for ma'amatz is: **Polls ship without a priority designation.** When Lihu reads back N whiteboards, he has to construct the "which of these is the load-bearing one" judgment himself, post-hoc. The doctrine says: *the spawner should have committed to that before firing*.

Concrete cost: re-read this round's `mishima/whiteboard.md`. It runs ~190 lines, deeply researched, and *correctly* declares that the cluster finding is "team templates omit purpose clause." That's the click. But the charter that spawned mishima didn't tell the professor "you are the ma'amatz rashi of this round; matara/ma'arach/mo'ed are supporting." Nor did it tell mishima "your finding will be the synthesizer's anchor." So mishima's professor had to *also* decide their own priority weight inside the round — work that should have been the orchestrator's.

The same gap exists in `cell-team`, `build-orchestrate`, and `liaison` — they coordinate parallel/sequential workers but don't name a ma'amatz rashi among them.

## BOTTOM — proposal

Three options were on the table per the charter. **I propose (b): sharpen the rnd skill's decomposition step to name the main angle.** Reasoning below, including why I rejected (a) and (c).

### Why (b) — sharpen rnd's decompose step

**Shape.** Add to `.claude/skills/rnd/SKILL.md` §1 (Decompose into independent angles) a new sub-step: *designate the ma'amatz rashi*. Proposed verbatim addition (≤6 lines):

> *Once you've picked N angles, name **one** as the ma'amatz rashi (מאמץ ראשי — main effort). The ma'amatz is the angle whose finding the round's recommendation will lean on most heavily; its charter gets the deepest read-first list and the most experienced model. The other N−1 are *ma'amtzei-ezer* (supporting efforts) — their charters are tighter, their job framed as "set conditions for the synthesis" rather than "produce a finding of equal weight." If you cannot name one as the ma'amatz, that's a tell that the question hasn't been decomposed yet — re-decompose. (IDF doctrine: every operation has exactly one main effort; "two main efforts" = no decision. Cross-ref: Clausewitz R5, principle 05 #6.)*

**Location.** `.claude/skills/rnd/SKILL.md` §1, between the empirical-sizing paragraph and the "Critical: pre-decompose at the orchestrator" paragraph. The skill's lifecycle already has "decompose → charter → fan out → synthesize"; ma'amatz designation is a sub-step of decompose, not a new phase.

**Companion edit (smaller).** Sam's synthesizer charter (in `usegin/teams/rnd-team.md`) should be told the ma'amatz designation up front — synthesis weights the ma'amatz finding as the anchor and reads supporting findings *against* it. Currently Sam treats all whiteboards as peers, which means the synthesizer has to re-derive priority post-hoc. (This is a separate, smaller team-template edit; flagged to coordinate, not edited.)

**Why this and not the others.**

### Why I rejected (a) — new charter block "Main effort / supporting efforts"

The charter skill's seven blocks are *per-spawn* — they describe one sub-Gin's shape. Ma'amatz is a *cross-spawn* property — it lives at the orchestrator's decomposition step, not inside any one charter. Adding a "main effort" block to the charter skill would require every charter to declare its own main-effort status, which is the wrong layer (the charter doesn't know whether it's a peer or main effort — the spawner does). Charter §1 (Purpose) already carries the political object; adding a peer/main-effort flag there confuses two things.

The charter skill is already well-shaped per the mishima professor's analysis. Don't perturb it.

### Why I rejected (c) — new methodology zettel

A zettel is the right shape for *recurring meta-patterns* (z023, z027, z029) — load-bearing claims that show up across many skills. Ma'amatz *is* such a pattern, but it currently has exactly one operational instantiation in our system (the rnd skill's parallel decomposition). z015 (pre-game manual — only systematize what we've done by hand once) says don't write the zettel until the pattern shows up in 2+ skills. Right now: rnd has it (gap), `cell` has it (gap), `build-orchestrate` has it (gap), `liaison` has it (gap) — all *latent*, none yet *practiced* with explicit ma'amatz. The pre-game manual rule says: land the discipline *in the skill that will use it first*, see whether the discipline is load-bearing, *then* extract the zettel.

**Predicted future move (do not do now):** when ma'amatz has been practiced inside rnd 2–3 times and works, lift it into a zettel that `cell` / `build-orchestrate` / `liaison` can adopt. Until then, keeping the discipline scoped to rnd is the disciplined move.

### What I am NOT claiming

- I am **not** claiming ma'amatz fully overlaps with Clausewitz R5 / principle 05 #6. It doesn't. R5 ranks *kinds of COG* across the whole UseGin operating model; ma'amatz designates *one operation's* main effort across its parallel branches. Different layer, different temporal scope.
- I am **not** claiming the existing principle 05 #6 should be promoted from principle to per-charter block. The charter skill's block 1 (Purpose) already carries the political object; promoting COG to a separate per-charter block would duplicate work. The right place is the *spawner* skills, not the charter meta-skill.
- I am **not** claiming we should adopt the IDF's "deliberate shift of main effort during operation" discipline — yet. Our R&D rounds are short enough (hours, not days) that mid-round re-designation hasn't been observed as friction. For longer-running orchestrations (`build-orchestrate` over multi-slice work), it may matter; flag for the matara/mo'ed professors to look at.

### Hebrew-language anchor terms (load-bearing where the Hebrew is the right word)

- **מאמץ** (*ma'amatz*) — effort. The third M.
- **מאמץ ראשי** (*ma'amatz rashi*) — main effort. The Schwerpunkt-equivalent. Singular per echelon per moment.
- **מאמצי-עזר** (*ma'amtzei-ezer*) — supporting efforts. Plural. Their job is to set conditions for the ma'amatz rashi.
- **תיעדוף** (*ti'eduf*) — prioritization. The line in the *pkudat krav* that allocates resources between ma'amatz rashi and ma'amtzei-ezer; my read is this is the canonical Hebrew term but I'm projecting from structure rather than from a primary source (see Friction #1).
- **פקודת קרב** (*pkudat krav*) — combat order. The artifact a ma'amatz designation lives inside.
- **כֹּבֶד מַשְׁקָל** (*koved mishkal*, "weight-of-importance") — occasional Hebrew rendering of *Schwerpunkt*; less common than *ma'amatz rashi* in modern IDF usage.
- **זה העיקר** / **כזה כזה** (*zeh ha-ikar* / *ke-ze ke-ze*, "this is the main thing" / "this and that") — the colloquial signal Israeli officers use to flag that an order *did* vs. *did not* commit to a main effort. Used in 2006-Lebanon AAR critique.
