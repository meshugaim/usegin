## On wiki failure (HN thread, dev community, others)

> "The big issue, regardless of the platform, has been getting people to
> care about documentation." — al_borland, HN

> "Out-of-date documentation can be worse than no documentation at all
> when it's actively wrong." — freedomben, HN

> "I have yet to actually use a platform with a good search
> functionality." — freedomben, HN

> "Forcing tribal knowledge to be written documented is the issue." —
> Rastonbury, HN

> "The sheer amount of information becomes unnavigable and unorganizable
> really easily." — flawstick, HN

> "What becomes everyone's responsibility effectively becomes no one's
> responsibility." — practitioner consensus, multiple sources

> "Treating knowledge as a destination" — recurring framing across
> Notion's own marketing, DEV community, and the "wiki is a graveyard"
> piece. The destination-vs-flow distinction is the unifying explanation.

## On the economics of capture

> Engineers spend an average of 6.5 hours per week looking for
> information or duplicating work — GitLab research, cited in DX and
> fullscale.io coverage. Other estimates: 3–10 hours/week.

> "Tribal knowledge adds 23 hours per developer per month in
> knowledge-seeking time. When key engineers leave, teams lose
> institutional context that takes 3–6 months to rebuild." — Glue blog
> on tribal knowledge.

> "The problem with internal documentation isn't usually that people
> don't know it's important — they do. It's that they don't have the
> time, they don't have the incentive, and they don't see other
> engineers spending time on docs. It's a culture problem." — DX,
> "Motivating developers to care about documentation"

## On Stripe's writing culture

> Patrick Collison's emails "literally had footnotes and were structured
> like research papers." — Pragmatic Engineer

> "Stripe creates sample documents so engineers aren't forced to stare
> at a blank page, which can be terrifying." — Slab

> "Friction logs document the end-to-end user flow, with a 'friction
> logger' getting in the mindset of a user and noting all friction
> points as well as ideas for enhancing UX." — Slab

> "Many of Stripe's most effective Product/Engineering teams published
> decision logs into their team Slack channels." — Slab

## On Basecamp Heartbeats

> "Heartbeats summarize the last ~6-weeks of work for a given team,
> department, or individual, and are written by the lead of the group
> and meant for everyone in the company to read."

> "They automatically create a library of progress — a collection of
> institutional knowledge, with day-by-day documentation by dozens of
> individual authors in their own words."

> "Long-form writing is an asynchronous form of communication, [so]
> people can publish thoughts when they're ready, and others can read
> them, consider them, and respond to them when they have time — with
> no synchronization of schedules required." — Jason Fried

## On post-mortems

> "A blameless postmortem stays focused on how a mistake was made
> instead of who made it." — PagerDuty

> "Organizations with mature postmortem cultures experience 50% fewer
> repeat incidents and recover 43% faster from outages." — DevOps.com
> citing industry studies

> Blameless culture "originated in the healthcare and avionics
> industries where mistakes can be fatal." — DevOps.com

## On ADRs

> "ADRs serve two purposes: they act as a record of decisions, allowing
> people months or years later to understand why the system is
> constructed in a particular way, and the act of writing them helps
> clarify thinking, particularly with groups of people." — multiple
> sources, originally Cognitect / Nygard

> "ADRs create an institutional memory that outlives individual team
> members, capturing nuanced context that traditional documentation
> often misses, which becomes particularly valuable during team
> transitions." — AWS Architecture Blog

## On Amazon's 6-page memo

> "The narrative structure of a good memo forces better thought and
> better understanding of what's more important than what, and how
> things are related, whereas PowerPoint presentations somehow give
> permission to gloss over ideas, flatten out any sense of relative
> importance, and ignore the interconnectedness of ideas." — Bezos,
> via multiple secondhand accounts

> "Meetings start with each attendee sitting and silently reading a
> 'six-page, narratively-structured memo' for about the first 30
> minutes." — Quartr

## On GitLab's handbook-first culture

> The handbook is "the single source of truth in the organization" and
> "any change must be documented first before being communicated or
> implemented." — GitLab handbook

> "Look it up in the handbook" before asking colleagues — GitLab norm.

## On Luhmann / Zettelkasten

> ~90,000 handwritten notes over 40 years, producing ~60 books and ~600
> publications.

> Three rules per card: "one atomic idea per card; a unique alphanumeric
> identifier; explicit links (written references to the identifiers of
> related cards)."

> "Luhmann did not copy quotations into his Zettelkasten; he restated
> ideas in his own language, which forced genuine understanding rather
> than passive collection."

> "Most practitioners report that the system starts to produce
> unexpected connections after a few hundred notes."

## On AI authorship and trust

> "96% of developers don't fully trust AI-generated code to be
> functionally correct." — Medevel, citing industry surveys

> "61% of developers warn that AI-generated code 'often produces code
> that looks correct but isn't reliable.'" — same

> "Adding an AI teammate often reduces team coordination,
> communication, and trust, and human-AI teams frequently underperform
> due to poor team cognition and mutual understanding." — Springer
> review

> "Attribution mechanisms link specific parts of model output to
> verifiable data or publications, enabling human collaborators to
> trace and validate responses." — IBM Research

> "In co-creative writing, participants described their sense of
> ownership in spectrum-like terms, suggesting a more fluid perception
> of ownership during AI-assisted creation." — co-creative writing
> research

> Stack Overflow Developer Survey 2025: ~25% of developers mostly use
> AI, ~27% partially use AI, for creating or maintaining documentation.
