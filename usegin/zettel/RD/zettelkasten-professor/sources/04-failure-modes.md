# Failure modes

## Collector's Fallacy (zettelkasten.de)

The mistaken belief that *accumulating* information equates to *acquiring
knowledge*. Bookmarking, photocopying, snipping into Notion = immediate
gratification of "I've got that now". But:

> "kept isn't read"

Reading alone leaves only temporary knowledge; once forgotten, it's as if
the reading never happened. The cure is short iterative cycles of
research → reading → assimilation, with strict time-boxes. Process material
on the way *in*, not in some indefinite "later".

This is the foundational failure mode. Every other Zettelkasten failure is
a flavor of this one — accumulating items the box pretends to hold but the
mind has not actually integrated.

## Hierarchies fail for thinking — Matuschak

> "By presorting things into well-specified categories, we necessarily fuzz
> their edges. Items often belong in multiple places... yet hierarchies
> demand single placement. Further sorting requires undoing existing
> structure."

This is why Luhmann rejected topical filing. Hierarchies *force premature
commitment* and *resist re-shaping*. By the time you realize the structure
is wrong, you have invested in it heavily.

Vannevar Bush:
> "The human mind does not work that way. It operates by association. With
> one item in its grasp, it snaps instantly to the next..."

## Modern Zettelkasten pitfalls (synthesized from sources)

- **Tag soup** — using tags as a substitute for the harder work of placing
  and linking.
- **Orphan zettels** — notes nobody ever links to. They functionally don't
  exist. The cure is the discipline of "you don't write a permanent note
  unless you can link it to at least one neighbor."
- **Link rot from over-broad notes** — when a note is too broad, links to it
  become ambiguous. Future-you can't tell *which* part of the note is being
  invoked.
- **Treating the box as archive, not interlocutor** — never re-reading,
  never letting old notes resurface. The box only generates surprise if you
  actually re-engage with it.
- **The "perfect first note" trap** — refusing to write because the system's
  rules feel intimidating. Better to write a bad permanent note that gets
  revised than no note. The box gets better through use.

## LessWrong author's own observation after years of practice

> the system becomes "stale" after months; better to start fresh rather than
> extend old structures

A real cost. A live Zettelkasten is *worked* — not just *kept*. Without
re-engagement, the box accumulates dead weight.
