# Structure notes, hub notes, and the three-layer model

## Three layers of a Zettelkasten (zettelkasten.de)

1. **Content notes** — individual zettels. The substrate. Below ~1000–1500
   notes, full-text search and tags are enough to navigate.
2. **Structure notes** — emerge as the archive grows. They function as
   *tables of contents* over a thread or domain. They are themselves notes
   in the box, not external metadata.
3. **Top-level structure notes** — structure notes that organize other
   structure notes. The umbrellas.

The layers are not designed up-front. They appear when navigation by
links + search starts to fail.

## Hub notes vs structure notes (Bob Doto)

Often conflated; Doto separates them:

- **Hub note** — for *discovery*. "A jumping-off point" that points to where
  particular trains of thought can be found. You consult it to *find* what's
  going on in some area.

- **Structure note** — for *development*. Where you "organize and structure
  the ideas that make up the trains of thought" toward a writing argument
  or a decision. You build one when you've identified an aspect worth
  developing and want to arrange related ideas into a coherent shape.

Doto's example workflow:
1. Consult hub note "PKM and Zettelkasten" — it points to several trains of
   thought.
2. Pick one that's interesting.
3. Create a structure note that pulls the relevant zettels together,
   rearranges them, makes connections explicit, adds your own analysis.
4. The structure note becomes a draft outline for an actual essay.

## Why this matters for design

Hub notes and structure notes are NOT the same thing as "categories" or
"folders". They are themselves zettels — they evolved out of the network as
useful entry points and useful organizing acts. They get linked to and
linked from like any other note.

Implication: a system that pre-bakes "categories" or "tags" as schema is
already on the wrong foot. A system that allows arbitrary zettels to
function as hubs (when they grow into that role) is on the right foot.
