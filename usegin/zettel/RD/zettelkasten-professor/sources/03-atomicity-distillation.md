# Atomicity and distillation

## Atomicity — Andy Matuschak

> "Notes which are only about one thing — but which, as much as possible,
> capture the entirety of that thing."

No clean litmus test exists. It's a tradeoff:
- **Too broad** → "you might not notice when you encounter some new idea
  about one of the notions contained within, and links to that note will be
  muddied."
- **Too fragmented** → "you'll also fragment your link network, which may
  make it harder to see certain connections."

Atomicity exists *in service of linking and re-use*. It's the same instinct
as separation-of-concerns in software: small, well-named modules compose;
god-classes don't.

## Titles as APIs — Matuschak

A note's title becomes an *abstraction handle*. "The entire note's ideas
can then be referenced using that handle."

Concrete: instead of titling a note "Medium Potential", title it "Enacted
experiences have incredible potential as a mass medium" — a complete claim.

Why this matters:
1. The title forces you to *commit to what the note actually asserts* — you
   can't fudge with a vague topic word.
2. Other notes can link to it as if calling a function with a clear contract.
3. As your library grows, you can build notes that "abstract over
   increasingly large subtrees" by linking many claim-titled notes together.

Topic-words ("Medium Potential") are like variable names; claim-titles
("Enacted experiences have incredible potential as a mass medium") are like
function signatures.

## Distillation in light of neighbors — Ahrens

The load-bearing operation is not capture, it's elaboration:
> "How does the new information contradict, correct, support, or add to what
> I already know?"

A permanent note is written *against* the existing box. Forte:
> "we must copy, translate, re-write, compare, contrast, and describe a new
> idea in our own terms."

> "Writing is not the outcome of thinking; it is the medium in which
> thinking takes place."

This is why the act of placing/distilling a zettel against its neighbors is
the load-bearing cognitive operation, not the act of writing it down.
Capture without elaboration = collector's fallacy.

## Ahrens' rules for permanent notes

1. Be extremely selective in what you decide to keep.
2. Keep the overall note as short as possible.
3. Use your own words; don't copy quotes verbatim.
4. Write down the bibliographic details on the source.
5. Compose them "as if you are writing for someone else" — full sentences,
   explicit references.

## Common Ahrens mistakes

- **Topic-based filing** → fragmentation. File by *context in which the note
  will be used*, not by what it's "about".
- **Isolated tips and tricks** without an integrated system → clutter.
- **Ignoring contradictions** → confirmation bias. *Save* contradictory and
  paradoxical ideas; they're high-value.
