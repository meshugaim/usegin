## Human side

War research SYNTHESIS D1: Modern Application argues Sun Tzu *is* our doctrine (small team, asymmetric, dislocate-before-confront — flank where giants aren't fortified). Clausewitz prof argues the opposite (friction, fog, trinity, COG, dialectic-holding — the *physics of the fight* once contact is made).

## UseGin side

Both are right at different layers; collapsing to one is the failure mode. Hold the dialectic (principle 7).

**Sun Tzu governs product/competitive strategy** — *where* we fight, what terrain we choose, when we deceive vs. confront. Effi-grounds-in-team-data is the indirect approach in business register: a flanking line on terrain where the giants aren't fortified. This is the doctrine for shipping, positioning, and any "what should we build" call.

**Clausewitz governs execution under uncertainty** — *what happens once a sub-Gin is in motion*, how friction accumulates, why the trinity (intent + agent stochasticity + Lihu's emotion) must stay alive, why the COG is Lihu's attention. This is the doctrine for orchestrating the work that's in flight.

**Decision rule**: when the question is *"what do we build / where do we compete,"* reach for Sun Tzu. When the question is *"how do we manage the work that's in flight,"* reach for Clausewitz. The trap is forcing one doctrine to do both jobs — Sun Tzu's ambushes don't help once you're already in the firefight; Clausewitz's friction doesn't tell you whether you should have entered this engagement at all.

The two layers stack: pick the right battle (Sun Tzu) → fight the friction-heavy battle well (Clausewitz). Most of UseGin's day-to-day is layer 2; layer 1 fires at spec and slicing time.
