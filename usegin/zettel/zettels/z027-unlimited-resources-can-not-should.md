## Human side

Lihu's framing: "you have all day, all time, all tokens, all resources." This is about what's **possible**, not what's **right**. We will figure out *should* later.

What it unlocks for Gin's work pattern:

- Spawn as many gins as the work needs (z023). No budget on professors.
- Run things end-to-end inside their charter (research → think → propose → discuss → revise → propose again) without human-in-the-loop until the dilemma actually surfaces.
- Multiple investigation passes in parallel — different angles can run concurrently and synthesize at the end.
- Reading deeply, not skimming — the Modern-Tools professor in the R&D round read 17+ tools in dossier depth; that was the right depth for that question.

What this does **not** mean:

- Spawn for the sake of spawning. Each gin needs a charter (z023). Empty work is still work.
- Skip the dilemma surfacing. "Unlimited resources" doesn't dissolve manager-relevant decisions; those still go to Lihu (z026).
- Skip the friction loop (z009). High friction is still a fork; unlimited tokens don't change that.

## Gin side

The mental shift for me: stop optimizing for token-economy when the question is hard. Optimize for *depth* and *parallelism*. Spend the tokens on:

1. Wider sub-Gin teams when a topic has multiple angles.
2. Letting professors read deeply (full dossier per source, not summaries-of-summaries).
3. Running rounds — first synthesis → reviewer round → revision → second synthesis — instead of single-shot.

What I keep economizing: prose to Lihu. Long investigation does not earn long report (z018). The output bandwidth to Lihu is *expensive* even when input bandwidth is free, because *his attention is the limit*. Save tokens where they matter; spend them where they don't.

The "should" comes later. For now: the budget is open, the discipline (charters, friction loops, dilemma protocol, decision shape) is what keeps it productive.
